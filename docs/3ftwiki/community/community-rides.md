Community bike rides are a powerful way to bring people together, promote cycling as a fun and practical mode of transportation, and create lasting connections within a neighborhood. Whether you're organizing a small, casual ride or a large-scale event, thoughtful planning and leadership are key to making the experience safe, inclusive, and engaging for everyone involved.

# The Power of Community Rides

Cycling isn't just about pedaling from point A to B—it's about freedom, connection, and empowerment. When we ride together, we create a movement (literally and figuratively) that inspires others to join. But let's be real: leading a group ride isn't just about picking a route and hoping for the best. It takes planning, a touch of courage, and a whole lot of heart.

The good news? You don't have to be a professional organizer to do this. If you can ride a bike, you can lead a ride. Start small, learn as you go, and build a community around something that makes people feel alive.

# Steps to Planning an Inclusive and Engaging Ride

## 1. Define the Purpose and Audience

- What's the goal? Social ride, advocacy ride, skills-building, or just a fun community roll?
- Who's invited? Families, commuters, beginners, experienced riders?
- Set the ride tone: fast-paced, leisurely, beginner-friendly, themed (e.g., a full moon ride, a coffee crawl, or a historical tour).
- Consider how the ride's purpose will affect your planning, promotion, and execution.

## 2. Plan the Route with Safety and Accessibility in Mind

- **Choose wisely**: The route is arguably the most critical factor in whether participants will return for future rides.
- Select a route that matches your riders' abilities and the stated purpose of the ride.
- Prioritize low-traffic streets, protected bike lanes, and dedicated paths when possible.
- Consider accessibility: ensure that the route works for adaptive cyclists and families with kids.
- Identify rest stops, bathroom locations, and water refill points.
- **Pre-ride the route when possible**: Check for construction, hazards, or challenging sections that might need special attention.
- Plan alternate routes or detours in case of unexpected road closures or safety concerns.
- For first-time rides, start with shorter, more manageable routes (5-10 miles) and expand as your community grows.

## 3. Check Legal and Insurance Needs

- More details are available on the [Insurance Page](/community/insurance).
- Small, casual rides can be organized by anyone, but as rides grow, consider liability coverage.
- Some starting or ending venues may require a certificate of insurance listing them as additionally insured.
- Ensure compliance with local traffic laws and city permits if required.
- Consider working with established cycling organizations that may already have insurance coverage.

## 4. Assemble and Prepare Your Ride Leadership Team

- More details on training your team and responsibilities are available on the [Ride Support Page](/community/ride-leadership)
- **Ride Leader(s):** Set the pace, make key decisions, and are responsible for the overall ride experience.
- **Sweeper(s):** Stay at the back to ensure no one is left behind or struggles alone.
- **Marshals:** Control crossings, manage traffic interactions, and keep the group together.
- **Mechanic Support:** Optional but helpful for quick roadside fixes.

### Leadership Team Communication
- Establish a reliable communication system between ride leaders, sweepers, and marshals:
  - **Walkie-talkies**: Affordable and effective for immediate communication without phone distractions or in areas with poor reception.
  - **Group chat**: Create a dedicated ride leaders' discord channel.
  - **Bluetooth earbuds**: Single-ear setups allow leaders to stay in communication while remaining aware of traffic.
  - **Hand signals and whistles**: Establish a simple system of signals for common situations.
- Practice your communication system before the event.
- Ensure all leaders have each other's phone numbers as backup.

### Leadership Visibility
- Ride leaders should wear high-visibility vests, bright shirts, or other distinctive items that make them easily identifiable to participants.
- Consider an inexpensive pack of printed vests or shirts for regular events (e.g., "Ride Leader," "Sweeper," "Ask Me for Help").
- Explain to participants how to identify ride leaders at the pre-ride orientation.

## 5. Promote the Ride Effectively

- More details on our [event marketing page](/community/marketing-guide)
- Create an event page (Facebook, Mixily, Meetup, etc.) with clear information about the ride's purpose, difficulty level, and what to bring.
- Use flyers, local bike shops, community boards, and spoke cards.
- Engage social media: stories, reels, countdowns, and personal invitations work wonders.
- **Word-of-mouth is crucial**: 
  - Encourage participants to bring friends through "Bring-a-Friend" incentives.
  - Ask previous riders to personally invite others in their network.
  - People are much more likely to try something new when invited by someone they know.
- Partner with local businesses, schools, or community organizations to reach new audiences.
- Be clear about what makes your ride special and why people should join.

## 6. Pre-Ride Orientation & Safety Briefing

- Arrive at least 30 minutes early to prepare and welcome early arrivals.
- Before the ride begins, set expectations and ensure everyone is on the same page.
- Provide a safety brief such as the League of American Bicyclists' 5 Need-to-Know Items [found in the Smart Cycling Manuals](https://shop.bikeleague.org/collections/bike-education-materials-1) or 3 Feet Please's simplified [**ALERT System**](/education/ALERT).

### Community Building at the Start
- **Use name tags**: For rides under 30 people, provide simple stick-on name tags to help people connect.
- **Introduction circle**: For smaller rides (under 15 people), take 5 minutes for quick introductions.
- Start with an icebreaker question like "What's your favorite thing about cycling?" or "How did you hear about today's ride?"
- Introduce all ride leaders and their roles.

### Key points to cover in your safety briefing:
- Introduce Ride Leaders and how to identify them
- Route Overview with highlights and potential challenges
- Rules of the Road and group riding etiquette
- Group rides verbal and hand signals communication
- How to ride predictably in a group
- What to do if there's an emergency or if you need to leave the ride early
- **Road sharing philosophy**: Emphasize respectful road sharing with pedestrians and drivers (details below)

## 7. Establish a Road Sharing Philosophy

- **Foster positive road sharing**: Explicitly address how the group will interact with other road users.
- Emphasize that antagonizing drivers or pedestrians is prohibited and counterproductive to cycling advocacy.
- Explain why hostile interactions can:
  - Make other participants uncomfortable
  - Create unsafe situations for the group
  - Damage public perception of cyclists
  - Potentially trigger road rage incidents that might affect other cyclists later
- Encourage friendly waves to courteous drivers and leading by example.
- Practice "taking the lane" safely and as a group when necessary, without aggressive behavior.
- Have a plan for addressing problematic behavior within your group.

## 8. Lead with Confidence and Adaptability

- Start with an upbeat tone—your energy sets the mood!
- Keep the pace manageable and check in with riders.
- Be flexible—unexpected detours or mechanical issues happen.
- Celebrate the journey, not just the destination.
- Model positive behavior in all interactions with other road users.
- Check in with new riders periodically to ensure they're comfortable.

## 9. Post-Ride Engagement

- Thank riders and encourage them to share their experiences.
- Connect riders to other groups or people in your area that may match their ability and interests, for example Racing Groups for ambitious riders, Major Taylor for cyclists of color, and youth groups for families.
- Gather feedback: What went well? What could be improved?
- Build a community: Set up a follow-up ride or create a group chat.
- Share photos and tag attendees to build excitement for the next ride.
- Send a follow-up email or message with information about the next ride.
- Celebrate achievements: distance covered, hills climbed, or simply completing the ride.

# Step-by-Step Action List for a Successful Ride

## Pre-Ride Planning (2-4 Weeks Before)

- [ ] Define the ride's purpose and audience
- [ ] Map out an accessible, safe, and engaging route
- [ ] Ride the route in advance to check for hazards or tricky areas
- [ ] Confirm legal and insurance needs
- [ ] Gather a leadership team with clear roles (ride leader, sweeper, marshals, mechanic)
- [ ] Create and distribute a ride announcement with time, date, route, and expectations
- [ ] Set up communication systems for the leadership team
- [ ] Acquire high-visibility identifiers for ride leaders
- [ ] Promote through social media, local businesses, and word-of-mouth
- [ ] Create a contingency plan for weather, emergencies, or other unexpected situations
- [ ] Prepare name tags if using them

## Week-of Preparation

- [ ] Send a reminder to registered participants
- [ ] Check weather forecasts and prepare any necessary updates
- [ ] Confirm all ride leaders and their roles
- [ ] Prepare any handouts, maps, or materials needed
- [ ] Test communication equipment
- [ ] Re-ride the route if possible to check for new hazards
- [ ] Gather essential repair tools including spare tubes, patch kits, CO2 cartridges or a mobile pump, tire levers, multi-tool, and wrenches
- [ ] Clearly communicate whether ride leaders will provide mechanical assistance or if riders are responsible for their own repairs

## Day-of Ride Preparation

- [ ] Arrive at least 30 minutes early to check-in riders and answer questions
- [ ] Inspect the start area and initial route for any last-minute hazards or changes
- [ ] Set up a welcome/check-in area
- [ ] (optional) Have a sheet to collect names, emails for future marketing, emergency contacts, etc.
- [ ] (optional) Collect liability waivers
- [ ] Distribute name tags and encourage introductions
- [ ] Offer to teach riders how to conduct an ABC Quick Check as they arrive
- [ ] Conduct a pre-ride orientation using the [ALERT system](/education/ALERT)
- [ ] Distribute any ride materials such as cue sheets or route maps
- [ ] Ensure all ride leaders and sweepers have each other's contact info and testing communications
- [ ] Check that riders have helmets, lights (as needed), and working bikes
- [ ] Take a group photo before departing

## During the Ride

- [ ] Start on time and set a friendly, steady pace
- [ ] Use clear, loud communication at intersections and turns
- [ ] Assign marshals to direct riders at key points if necessary
- [ ] Watch for struggling riders and adjust pace or take breaks as needed
- [ ] Keep the group together, using a sweeper to ensure no one is left behind
- [ ] Handle mechanical issues quickly, assigning a ride leader or mechanic to assist
- [ ] Stay aware of traffic conditions and modify the route if needed

## Post-Ride

- [ ] Thank participants and celebrate the ride’s success
- [ ] (optional) Post a donation sheet with a QR code to your donation page or a tip jar.
- [ ] Offer a social gathering spot post-ride if possible (e.g., coffee shop, park)
- [ ] Gather feedback from riders on what they liked and what could improve
- [ ] Share photos and stories on social media and tag attendees
- [ ] Encourage riders to sign up for future rides or join a cycling group
- [ ] Document lessons learned and any changes needed for future rides
- [ ] Plan the next ride and keep the momentum going!

## Optional / Extra

- [ ] **First-Aid Kit:** Have a small kit on hand and ensure a leader is trained in basic first aid.
- [ ] **Refreshments:** If it’s a bigger event, coordinate with local businesses or volunteers for water stops.
- [ ] **Rain Date / Cancellation Notice:** Clearly communicate how participants will be notified in case of cancellation or postponement.
- [ ] **Pace Groups:** For larger rides, consider splitting into slow, moderate, and fast groups, each with its own leader.

# Equipment & Supplies Checklist

Having the right equipment ensures that your community ride runs smoothly and safely. Below is a sample checklist to get started.

## Ride Leader Essential
- [ ] High-visibility vests or shirts for ride leaders, sweepers, and marshals  
- [ ] Printed cue sheets, route maps, or digital GPS route files  
- [ ] Walkie-talkies or Bluetooth communication devices for leadership team  
- [ ] Emergency contact list for ride leaders (including local emergency services)  
- [ ] First-aid kit (bandages, antiseptic wipes, gloves, gauze, and an emergency blanket)  
- [ ] Basic bike repair toolkit (see below for details)  
- [ ] Whistles or bells for communication and crowd control  
- [ ] Name tags or stickers for participants (optional but great for community-building)  
- [ ] Contact sheet for all ride leaders and volunteers  
- [ ] Reflective tape or lights for evening rides  
- [ ] [Printed Emergency Protocols](/community/safety)

## Bike Repair & Maintenance Kit
- [ ] Spare tubes (various sizes)  
- [ ] Patch kits and tire levers  
- [ ] Portable pump or CO2 inflator with cartridges  
- [ ] Multi-tool with Allen keys, screwdrivers, and a chain tool  
- [ ] Adjustable wrench for bolt-on wheels  
- [ ] Chain lube and rags for quick fixes  
- [ ] Duct tape and zip ties for temporary repairs  

## Rider Comfort & Support
- [ ] Extra water bottles or hydration station (especially for longer rides)  
- [ ] Snack packs (granola bars, bananas, electrolyte tabs for hot weather)  
- [ ] Sunscreen and bug spray  
- [ ] Hand sanitizer and wet wipes  

## Community Engagement & Promotion
- [ ] Flyers or QR codes for upcoming rides or related advocacy efforts  
- [ ] A sign-in sheet or digital check-in system for tracking participants  
- [ ] Social media or email signup sheet for post-event follow-ups  
- [ ] Donation jar or digital tip jar QR code (if applicable)  

# Final Thoughts

Leading a community ride is about showing up, creating a space for people to connect, and making cycling feel accessible and fun. Be bold, be adaptable, and most importantly, enjoy the ride.

Now, grab your bike, rally your community, and start pedaling toward change!
